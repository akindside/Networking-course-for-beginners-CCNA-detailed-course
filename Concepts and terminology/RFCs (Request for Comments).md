**RFCs (Request for Comments)** are the official documents that define how the Internet actually works. If the **ISO** provides the theoretical "laws" (like the OSI Model), **RFCs** are the "blueprints" and "instruction manuals" written by the engineers who build the hardware and software.

They are published by the **IETF (Internet Engineering Task Force)** and cover everything from how an email is formatted to how your computer gets an IP address.

---

### The Evolution of an RFC

The name "Request for Comments" sounds casual because, in 1969, the first Internet pioneers didn't want to seem like they were "commanding" people. They were just sharing ideas. Today, however, an RFC is a formal, permanent standard.

1. **Internet-Draft:** Someone has an idea for a new protocol or a fix for an old one. This is a "rough draft."
    
2. **Review:** Engineers around the world test it, argue about it, and refine it.
    
3. **Publication:** Once everyone agrees, it is assigned a unique number (e.g., **RFC 791**) and is never changed. If it needs an update, a _new_ RFC is written to "obsolete" or "replace" the old one.
    

---

### Famous RFCs You Use Every Day

Every time you use the internet, you are using dozens of RFCs simultaneously:

- **RFC 791:** The definition of **IP** (Internet Protocol).
    
- **RFC 793:** The definition of **TCP**.
    
- **RFC 1034 / 1035:** How **DNS** (Domain Name System) works.
    
- **RFC 2616:** The original rules for **HTTP** (Web browsing).
    
- **RFC 5321:** How **Email** (SMTP) is sent.
    

### Why Are They Important for You?

- **Interoperability:** Because of RFCs, a router made by Cisco can talk to a server made by Dell and a phone made by Apple. They are all following the same "instruction manual."
    
- **The "Final Word":** If two engineers argue about how a protocol should work, they check the RFC. It is the ultimate source of truth in networking.
    
- **Open Access:** All RFCs are free and available to the public. Anyone can read them at https://ietf.org.
    

---

### Key Takeaway

If you are learning to code or do networking:

- **ISO/OSI** = The theoretical concept (The "What").
    
- **RFC** = The technical implementation (The "How").
